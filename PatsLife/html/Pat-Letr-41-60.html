1978 February 12

1721 Norris

Austin, TX 78704

Dear Eve,

Happy Valentine’s Day! The sun is out for the first time in weeks and it’s wonderful.

Sorry I didn’t write – been busy here. The day Mr. Waters was buried, our neighbor and friend Mr. [Woodie] Lankford, had a heart attack. He was in intensive care for almost a week, died, and was buried Saturday. Walt was a pallbearer. It all happened so fast – he’d not been ill, was 63, retired because of a back injury – that it’s hard to adjust to. He was a lot of help to Walt with Walt’s building projects, etc. Matthew liked to go over there to visit the Langford‘s - so it’s sad – we’ve tried to help a little – cooked dinner one night, etc., But wish there was more we could do for Sybil his wife. I’m sure we can help out just by visiting, etc. – she loves the kids.

Walt’s folks were here for a few days – Bette will get her BS in engineering this spring and is looking for a job that might get her a transfer to Egypt. No comment. Nor will I comment on Karen except – well not even an exception. But I hope they are happy.

These kids drive me crazy – does this last forever? Only the first 20 years? Write soon.

Love, Pat.

A work colleague, George King, who had begun Austin Veterans Administration work in the same 1975, 13 week training class, had bought a model home in Austin. The model home developer had converted the home's two car garage into sales office space, using four sets of sliding glass doors, two six foot and two eight foot. George had no need of an office, but he did want a garage for his cars. He wanted to be rid of the doors and I had the idea to enclose the front porch with the doors. I removed the Austin stone verneer from the enclosure, using the stone for corner posts at the supporting wrought iron columns. Then I put gypsum bord on the exterior-become-interior, wall papered it and laid tile on the slab floor continuing the illusion that what was once a front porch was now "interior".

It’s been raining here all day – and yesterday – I didn’t know there was so much rain! It is spring so I guess it’s usual, but it sure ruins a Sunday .

We are now the proud owners of a house and a car. We’ve had the car about three weeks (a red 81 Toyota Corolla) and I’m still not driving very often. These people drive on the other side of the road and when it comes time to make a right turn, I get totally confused. We move into our house on 5 December and are really looking forward to that. The house is very modern in design - (It’s about 10 years old) and is on a hillside – so while we don’t have a harbor view, we do have a terrific view of Karori [Matt outside on dining room deck with Karori in background]. We've moved to New Zealand. After years of dissatisfacction with President Ronald Reagan, and years of increased nuclear tensions, Pat and I concurred that we wanted to get as far away as we comfortably could from an area of likely nuclear attack. Austin had Bergstrom Air base near by plus some high tech industry and a major highway artery.

The very first few days were quite jarring. Databank had generously allowed us a two day stay in Hawaii, pleasantly cooler than the July the Austin we'd left. Cold, gale force winds persuaded us that in New Zealand, July means winter. Our bodies would not accept that autumn could last less than a day, traversing the Equater and Tropic of Capicorn by air.

For the first few months, Databank covered our housing cost in a Motel in Lower Hutt at 95-98 Pharazyn St. Databank were getting us on our way to cultural conversion by loading our initial larder with stuff like Marmite. The rail stop was a short walk away where I could commute to Downtown Wellingon on the narrow gauge train. Lower Hutt was sandwiched between Upper Hutt and Petone along the Hutt river.

Pat had to walk across the river to buy groceries and carry them back withut the convenience of a car.


Wellington is New Zealand's capitol, the nation has roughly 1/100 the population of the United States. Consequently central political figures were treated more casually, both by themselves and the populace. For example, I rubbed elbows with a Prime Minister while browsing at the garden store.

15 Lynmouth Ave.

Karori, Wellington 5, New Zealand

Dear Eve,

Well, another year gone – and I feel no wiser, do you?

It is summer now – early summer – and I’m finally beginning to believe it (my mind says Winter still). When the sun is out, it is just beautiful here – and I feel like a lizard crawling out to its warmth. It’s a little windy today. (it does get windy here – part of Wellington‘s charm which I may understand someday). It seems so unlike January – but then August didn’t seem like August either. One of these days my calendar will be in sync.

We love our house. It’s about 10 years old – and roomy, light, and warm. (It’s the first year we’ve had a fireplace to hang stockings by.) Walt is making a frame for a waterbed out of a native wood called Remu – it is very pretty and even put a headboard on it this time. he’s building the platform now, so hopefully we’ll be sleeping on it tonight. I finally got those oak chairs (the ones we had in the kitchen) sanded and varnished. They look great.

We have a new cat – well not new, secondhand I guess. We met a family who is moving to Malaysia and couldn’t take their nine month old cat Toffee. So after checking us out, they decided Toffee could adopt us. He’s a real sweet cat and has quite a loud purr.

The kids are out of school until February 4. Kate and Kris will be in Standard 3 at Karori Normal and Matthew will be in Form 3 at Wellington College.

Walt rides his bike to work every day – it’s a little harder since we moved up to this hill. Running is harder too, but I’ve gotten back to it – run far enough so that I can walk back up the hill instead of running.

Christmas Day was quiet here, but we did have a nice day. We spent the afternoon at the Tyler‘s (our neighbors on Campbell Street) both our old neighbors (Tyler’s) and our new ones (Crumps) got the kids presents – nothing big, but I thought that was very nice. K & K & M thought it was wonderful, of course. We went to a Databank picnic the week before Christmas. Father Christmas arrived in a jeep with presents for all the kids. Then there was a party at Walt’s office and the kids were given another present! Walt gave me a green stone (native Jade) necklace –

Enclosed (I hope) are a couple of pictures. Thanks for the one of Amy. There is a shelf in our bedroom where I put all the pictures of family and friends. Why don’t you send one of you and Mike?

How are Rusty and his wife Lisa doing?

We are doing fine – we miss everyone though,

Love.

Pat

Seasons are flipped in the antipoodes; months December thru March are summer. Winter cold (May thru September) blows forcefully from the southwest.

Rimu is beautiful, a little splintery. In New Zealand it's fine grain is exploited for mouldings and furniture. In the exploitative spirit of English Imperialism, resources like native woods were harvested with little regard to sustainabliity. The astonishingly majestic Kaori forests were reduced to frail remnants to make superb ships for Britain's wooden fleet.

Participating in native wood exploitation, I made my bed of Rimu, and added a heater to combat Wellington's chill.

I had ridden a bike to work in Ann arbor (10 miles one way), and in Austin (a more leisurly 4 miles each way); anticipationg a more difficult ride in New Zealand I had traded in my 10 speed for an 18 speed mountain bike. Good choice.

New Zealand had suffered an economic crisis in 1984 after the government had rationalized their ecomony. They had worked themselves into the ruinous position of subsidizing their main source of trade income. The recesson that followed rationalization meant that many of Databank's 1984 employees left for Australia or elsewhere in the English speaking world. Thus Databank was generous with incentives, making it easier for the family to pick -up and move. Databank were continuously attentive to staff morale; thus the many sponsored activities and parties. Can you believe free Steinlager at Friday staff meetings?

I’m gradually making a few good friends. This morning I’m going shopping with Sandy – a lady from England. David Humphrey (from Austin) finished his book about the history of Austin. It’s a big, beautiful well written book, which we were pleased to get – an autographed copy from for Christmas. It also got good reviews. Did you see it in the stores – or is it just in Austin – surely not.

We enjoyed the pictures you sent – anytime you have an extra – We’ll be happy to get one. We’ve been busy here at the house – putting trees, shrubs, etc., so I haven’t taken any pictures lately.

It’s been a pleasant summer here – although it’s still hard for me to think “summer“ and that next is Autumn. I guess it takes a few years.

I hope you’re all in good health and happy. Say hello to everyone. Love

Pat.

The sun streamed in so much that some exposed fabric was bleached and weakened. Ditto book jackets. Cooler ambient temperatures and commonly windy conditions cancelled our awareness of the sun's intensity. Then too, the protective ozone layer was more damaged in the southern hemisphere than in the northern. Meaning, we probably experienced more damaging UV rays than we would have in the northern hemisphere.


Gardening on level areas (Lower Hutt, 123 Campbell in Karori, had looked so easy. So many homes displayed delicate flowering plants. The steep slope of 15 Lynmouth, higher wind speeds and possibly its architectural, levelling fill material made for less forgiving gardening. I had a pretty high failure rate. I had no appreciation for the dessicating effects of wind.


The Databank travel bonuses were more of Databank's employee retention measures. New Zealand had long been an exporter of talent; many young wanted to free themselves from the insularity and separation of the far southwest Pacific, if only for a few years.

The kids are back in school for the second trimester. We go to Matthew's school [Wellington College, Wellington] Thursday night for conference with his teachers. And on Saturday the 14th, we are invited to a party that the parents of one of the students in Matthew’s home room class is giving – just to get to know each other – sounds like a good idea to me since I don’t know any of the kids or parents. Since I work at Kris and Kate ‘s school library and kitchen, I have met, some of those parents.

We did enjoy our trip to Mount Taranaki - did you get the card? We had never seen such beautiful birds as we saw at a park in New Plymouth. I didn’t know anything so intensely colorful existed.

We also took a walk part of the way to the top of Mount Taranaki. Matthew Kate and Kris set the pace of our walk and what the book said would take 2 1/2 to 3 hours, took us 59 minutes 30 seconds. Kris said at one point in a very surprised voice, “you know I’m actually enjoying this.“ This from someone who usually drags at the rear claiming sore feet, aching head, etc. was pleasant to hear.

Did I tell you that Mary and Jimmy GIll my sister are coming to see us in January. We are looking forward to that. I think we can come there for Christmas 87 – anyway, we hope so after Walt has worked at Data Bank two years, he gets an extra month’s salary and travel vouchers each year. I don’t know what the travel vouchers actually cover, but we (me) are hoping we can swing it - visit Fort Worth, Austin and Wisconsin.

Well, how are you all doing? It seems strange that it’s summer. There are strange that it’s not summer here after all it is June! Did Julie graduate? Did you visit Austin? Did you miss us? We miss having good friends come for the weekend.

I don’t understand what embarrassed you about writing down your birthdays and anniversaries(?) Did you ever see Jerry and Missy? Karen? Rusty? How is he doing with the new wife? With the old wives? I’m working on these needlepoint covers for our dining room chairs – I’m almost finished with the first one – only three to go! Must go – hope to hear from you soon.

Love,

Pat.

A nation is a lot of things.
When I was an American, some of those things were family and community and county and state and religion and education and identifications. That plane of abstractions rode, uneasily, in a Maxwellian field of terrain and soil and weather and flower and fauna. Those abstractions and field bobbed in a magnetic field of culture and history and tools of comprehension.

It was different being a Kiwi.
The family of my Father and Mother lived in Niederhochst, Deutschland; community and county and state and religion differed in ways I'd never experienced in 44 years of being an American. But jarringly different were terrain and soil and weather and flower and fauna.

A Polynesian Noah loaded not an ark, but a trimaran with flower and fauna known nowhere else but Aotearoa. North Island soil simmered with volcanic instability that colored Wellington architecture, both humble and proud. The BNZ tower building in wellington had not only vertical columns, but transverse columns, the better to endure tremblors; our Karori home was wired with both electrical and structural, tensile wiring to better the chances of crawling out of a quake shattered home.

The quick time march up the flanks of Taranaki and the lumpy fields of millenia old massive volcanic bombs on the drive from Palmerston North to New Plymouth park were echt Kiwi.

1986 July 17

15 Lynmouth Ave.

Karori, Wellington 5, New Zealand

Dear Eve,

I wonder when we will begin to associate winter with July! It’s not really freezing cold weather – I think there’s only been one morning below 32°F – but when the Southerlies blow and the rain comes down for days at a time, I think a nice quiet snowstorm would be nice. We do have wonderful views of winter though – in the distance are snow covered mountains [ Remutakas] that can be seen on sunny warm days like today. The warmer days of winter are wonderful and probably outnumbered the rainy cold ones.

Did you have a July 4 party? We had some friends (Argentinians and New Zealanders) over for dinner; I did serve apple pie. It was not really a celebration of independence, just sort of happened on that date.

We haven’t been doing anything exciting lately. I’ve been halfheartedly looking for a part-time job. I’ll either get a job – or go back to school. I’m not highly motivated at the present time, but I am trying a special semester at Victoria University in a couple of weeks[-] will rekindle old fires (they may be out by now). Speaking of fires we finally have a house with a fireplace – but it’s more for show than heat (a black freestanding "designer" one) so we have been looking at more efficient wood heaters – that also looked halfway decent. And we ran across a lady originally from Boston (she married a New Zealander 20 years ago) publishes a paper called “Energy Watch“. We went there to look at a stove “ugly as sin“ they were selling and then started talking about solar energy. She “Molly“ and her husband [a nuclear engineer, educated at UCLA) insisted we stay for lunch we -spent the whole afternoon with them – talking of this and that – they had wonderful books. It was very nice – and the children sat around reading books and petting cats.

Walt and Matthew went fishing Sunday – some guys from Walt’s office rented a fishing boat and captain for the day – Matthew caught a shark! We learned later that there are lots of sharks around and that’s the kind of fish in the fish and chips chops. Matthew found out that he’s not wild about fishing – but did enjoy being out in Cook Straight for the day – it was a wonderful sunny and calm day. Kris Kate and I elected not to go. We stayed home and the girls baked and frosted the cake with very little supervision for me.

Tonight we go to "Wellington College – on stage" a production put on by Matt's school - he will usher.

I’ve been having trouble when running with my knees – finally had it x-rayed, which showed no abnormalities. So this A.M. I went to a sports Doctor who said I have stretched a ligament and that my knee – cap is slipping – now I go to the physical therapist for 10 treatments, take anti-inflammatory pills for 10 days, rub a medicated cream on my knee and then will have to have special inserts in my shoes – and it’s all for free – can you believe it? All personal injuries are covered under the Accident Compensation Corporation. I sure have missed running these last three weeks and will be glad to start again soon.

alt and I go to take our written driving test next week – God I hate test! (Guess I'd better not go back to school) and already have sweaty palms just thinking about it.

Thanks for the pictures – one of these days we’ll have more. I did remember Matthew‘s class picture. Matt hates this picture – says he looks fat – he’s not. Kate and Kris won’t have their’s taken until October.

Love

Pat.

Surprising how cold 90 %+ humidity air at 32 - 34 degrees can feel. Sucks the warmth out of your bones. The rewards were the snow capped Rimutakas visible from our kitchen window. On adventuresome days, we could walk up to the top of Karori valley and see snow capped Southern Alps on the South Island, across Cook Strait.


New Zealand government actively encouraged vigorous activities. Likely this reflected the Public Health System's recognition that a vigorous public was a healthier (and cheaper to keep healthy) public. Sprains aches and breaks were the flip side of a more vigorous public.

Besides Molly's meal we got her great affection for Mont-Saint-Michel and Chartres (Penguin Classics) by Henry Adams and Ray Carney A demanding, but rewarding read.


The fishing craft was built during WW II. Wartime shortages meant the hull was mostly cement reinforced by steel. It lookes stout, rust bleeding through the concrete and somewhat ungainly. Fifteen to twenty Databank programmers and analysts crowded the deck. Tryking to cast a line midst the crowd, I succeeded in hitting Chris Studt on the head with a lead sinker. My limited enthusiasm for fishing quickly waned.

I had had a heart attack earlier that year (I believe it was that year) so the possibility that Pat could become a single Mom was not lost on us. Preparing to have an adequate income was a responsible preparation for possible future difficulties.

Moving to a different country, to a different job, to a different economy and different social mores meant unanticipated stresses. A few work colleagues were needlessly stressful.

My experience makes me more sympathetic to immigrants. To any country.

For a while after the attack, I stopped commuting to work by bicycle and took the bus. Eventually angina abated and I resumed bicycling.

1987 February 19

15 Lynmouth Ave.

Karori, Wellington 5, New Zealand

Dear Eve,

Finally, it has rained. It was quite dry here – fire bans, on barbecues, rubbish, burning, etc. Sometimes it does get dry toward March and the fire danger is great because of the wind, but this year started out dry. Anyway, we had a lot of hot, dry weather – enough so that I was glad to see it end.

Thank you for the birthday card; hope you got the one we sent you. Did you have a nice birthday. I did. Walt’s mother called (I can remember when that wouldn’t have seems so special) and so did Walt’s brother. Walt gave me a beautiful leather satchel – I think he’s trying to tell me something! – For school. Unfortunately another year came with the day too!

I finished reading Lake Woebegone Days and enjoyed it – it was great fun wasn’t it? I’ve loaned it to a friend a friend‘s mother[Margaret Huntington, mother of Peg Gilman] (American) who misses hearing the program on Saturday nights – she’s even been to the broadcast – says you need to get tickets four months in advance.

The kids are back in school have been since the first of this month. Kris was home today because of an earache. Kate missed eating lunch with Kris, but at least she went on to school without a fuss. I remember in the past, having to promise, the one who is not sick the moon practically before she would go on to school. Music lessons begin this Saturday. Kate still plays the cello; Kris the violin. Matthew is taking French horn lessons at his school – and is supposed to be checking on how to get in the orchestra, but unless someone comes up to him and gives him an engraved invitation, he probably won’t be in it. We liked hearing about the seeds – what a good idea to take them to the botanical gardens. If you need some more, let me know. Walt keeps putting in more trees here. Recently, he added a tulip tree and a Babaco. We’ve played 50 (small) trees and so far more have died – Well one did but the nursery replaced it. Can you believe the owner of the nursery came out to the house to check the tree – wanted to see why it died – it was an Australian wattle tree, which was replaced – but the replacement is not too happy either – must be the location.

I think when it finally expires, we put in a native palm tree. It would look good in front of the white wall. Are you getting excited about the wedding? Wish we could come.

By the way – when you send a package, you can count it taking two months, unless it’s mail which cost a fortune. The deadline for mailing packages to arrive at Xmas time is something like October 20. I don’t know what “class“ means just tell him you want to send it “surface” – that is if you mail me again. is Amy getting excited about graduation? What will you do when she leaves? When it seems strange – maybe a few more cats? I love that article you sent. Some days I can’t wait

Hope to hear from you soon

Love

Pat
